Project Overview

Project Overview

End-to-end Revenue Growth Management (RGM) modelling for Carlsberg Hong Kong — measuring how price, promotions, distribution and competition drive beer sales.

🎯 Objective

Estimate price elasticity and promotional elasticity at the PPG (Price Pack Group) level across two retail channels in Hong Kong, and derive actionable outputs for revenue planning.

Business Questions Answered

  • How much does volume change when price goes up / down by 1%?
  • How much uplift does a price promotion (TPR) generate?
  • How does a competitor's price or distribution change affect Carlsberg sales?
  • Of total sales, how much is "base" vs "promotional / incremental"?
  • If a competitor raises price by 10%, where does the volume flow?

1

Master_data.py — Data Preparation

Aggregates raw SKU-level scanner data to PPG level. Creates two versions: one with Promo Flag (used for base price derivation) and one without (used for modelling and competition mapping).

2

Competition.py — Competitor Mapping

For each PPG, identifies which other PPGs are "competitors" based on three rules: Beer Segment interaction, ABV Classification interaction, and Pack Size × Pack Count interaction. Outputs a binary competitor matrix fed directly into Modeling.py.

3

Feature Engineering.py — Variable Creation

Derives all model inputs: Base Price, TPR (discount depth), WTD (distribution), Seasonality Index, Time Trend, Stockpiling, Downsizing, OOS Ratio/Recovery, and promotional event flags (Lucky Draws, GWP, PWP).

4

Modeling.py — OLS → GDA Two-Step Process

Step 1 — OLS: Ordinary Least Squares is run first (unconstrained) to generate initial coefficient estimates ("priors") per PPG, stored in ModelEQ.xlsx (columns: Prior, SD).
Step 2 — GDA: Reads OLS priors from ModelEQ.xlsx and runs 100 constrained gradient descent iterations to refine them within economic bounds. Also calculates Base vs Incremental Sales.

5

Volume_Transferability.py — Post-Modelling Analysis

Uses modelling outputs to build Power BI-ready files: own elasticities, cross-price elasticities, cross-distribution elasticities, and volume transferability matrices (brand-brand & internal-external).

📊 Key Outputs at a Glance

Own Price Elasticity

% volume change per 1% price change for each PPG. Typically negative (−0.5 to −4.0).

Promo Elasticity (TPR)

Volume uplift from price promotions/discounts. Typically negative coefficient (more discount = more sales).

Cross-Price Elasticity

How a competitor's price change affects Carlsberg PPG volumes. Positive = substitutes.

Distribution Elasticity

Volume impact of gaining/losing shelf presence (Weighted Trading Distribution).

Base vs Incremental

Weekly split: how much volume is "base" (would exist without promos) vs "incremental" (driven by promotions).

Volume Transferability

If a competitor raises price by 10%, what % of their lost volume flows to each Carlsberg brand?

Key Concepts

Core terminology and methodology used throughout the codebase. Click each card to expand.

📦
PPG — Price Pack Group
The fundamental unit of modelling
▶
A PPG (Price Pack Group) groups multiple individual SKUs that share the same commercial identity: same Brand, Pack Type, Pack Size, and Pack Count. For example, all 330ml Carlsberg cans sold in a 6-pack are one PPG, regardless of which retailer stocks them.

Why PPG and not SKU? SKU-level data is too granular and noisy for meaningful elasticity estimation. Aggregating to PPG level ensures enough volume to fit statistically reliable models.

Model Flag == 1 means the PPG has enough volume/history to be modelled. PPGs with Model Flag == 0 are bucketed into "Others".
💰
Base Price vs Average Price
Separating the "real" price from the promoted price
▶
Average Price is simply Total Revenue ÷ Total Units sold in a week. It includes weeks when products are on promotion — so it's a blended price.

Base Price is the "regular" (non-promoted) price a product sells at. It represents what consumers pay when there is no active discount.

How is Base Price calculated? Using an 8-week rolling maximum of Average Price on non-promotional weeks, followed by two smoothing passes:
  1. Remove single-week "blips" (spikes surrounded by lower values)
  2. Cap small changes (≤1%) to avoid noise
Base Price ≈ max(Avg Price) over rolling 8-week non-promo window
Base Price is always ≥ Average Price (it's the ceiling). If Avg Price exceeds Base Price in any week, Base Price is corrected upward.
🏷️
TPR — Temporary Price Reduction
Measuring the depth of promotional discounts
▶
TPR captures how deep a price promotion is in a given week. It is computed as the discount from Base Price:
TPR = 1 − (Average Price / Base Price)
TPR = 0 means no promotion (selling at full Base Price). TPR = 0.20 means a 20% discount. A minimum threshold of 5% is applied — discounts smaller than 5% are treated as TPR = 0 (noise).

In modelling, LN_TPR = log(1 + TPR) is used as a variable. Its coefficient (elasticity) is expected to be negative: a larger discount → more sales.
TPR is calculated only when Total Units ≥ 8-week rolling mean (avoiding counting low-volume weeks as "promo weeks").
🏪
WTD — Weighted Trading Distribution
A measure of where the product is sold
▶
WTD (Weighted Trading Distribution) measures not just whether a product is stocked in a store, but the importance of those stores. It weights store presence by the store's share of total category sales.

A WTD of 80% means the product is available in stores that account for 80% of total beer sales in that channel.

LN_WTD (log of WTD) is the modelling variable. A positive coefficient means better distribution → higher sales. WTD is also smoothed (same 8-week rolling max method as Base Price) to remove noise from temporary distribution changes.
📈
Price Elasticity
How sensitive volume is to price changes
▶
Own Price Elasticity = % change in volume for a 1% change in own price. Because the model uses log-log specification:
log(Volume) = α + β₁·log(Base Price) + β₂·log(WTD) + ... + ε
β₁ = Own Price Elasticity
The coefficient of LN_Base_Units_Price is directly the own price elasticity. It must be negative (higher price → lower volume).

Bounds enforced: Own price elasticity is constrained to [−4.0, −0.01] to ensure economic sense.

Cross-Price Elasticity measures how a competitor's price change affects a Carlsberg PPG's volume. Positive cross-price elasticity = substitutes (if competitor raises price, consumers switch to Carlsberg).
🔻
Stockpiling
The post-promo demand dip
▶
During a promotion, consumers "stockpile" — buying more than they normally would. In the weeks after the promotion ends, they consume from their stockpile and buy less. This creates a negative demand effect.

How it's modelled: For each discount week (TPR > threshold), a decay function is applied over the next 1–3 weeks. Multiple decay rates (5% to 20%) are tested. The rate with the strongest negative correlation with actual sales is chosen per PPG.
StockPiling(t+k) = discount_flag(t) × (1 − decay_rate)^k
The stockpiling variable has a negative elasticity in the model (it depresses sales in post-promo weeks).
❌
OOS — Out of Stock
Lost sales due to unavailability
▶
When a product is not available in stores, sales fall below their potential. The OOS Ratio measures this lost distribution:

Step 1: Compute a "Transformed WTD" — the WTD on non-promo weeks (promo periods distort WTD upward).
Step 2: Track the ACV Threshold — the 30th percentile of normal WTD levels for each stable distribution period.
Step 3: OOS = max(0, ACV_Threshold − Transformed_WTD)
Step 4: OOS_Recovery = when OOS ratio is recovering (falling back toward threshold).
OOS_Ratio = OOS / 100
OOS elasticity is negative (out-of-stock → fewer sales). OOS Recovery is positive (recovering stock → more sales).
🤖
OLS → GDA Two-Step Modelling
How the model estimates elasticities
▶
Modelling uses a two-step process:
Step 1 — OLS (Ordinary Least Squares): Run first, unconstrained, to generate initial coefficient estimates ("priors") saved in ModelEQ.xlsx. OLS gives a data-driven starting point but can produce economically invalid signs.

Step 2 — GDA (Gradient Descent Algorithm): Reads OLS priors and refines them over 100 iterations with full economic constraints. Instead of a plain regression, this custom approach allows:
  • Enforcing economic sign constraints (e.g., own price must be negative)
  • Incorporating prior beliefs (from OLS estimates as starting points)
  • Handling collinearity between variables
How it works (100 iterations per PPG):
  1. Initialize: Set coefficients from OLS priors
  2. Predict: Compute predicted log-sales = α + Σ(coefficient × variable)
  3. Error: error = exp(Actual) − exp(Predicted)
  4. Correlation: Pearson correlation of each variable with the error term
  5. Update: Move each coefficient in the direction that reduces error, within its bounds
  6. Slowdown: If a coefficient oscillates (sign flips in consecutive iterations), apply a slowdown factor to stabilize
New Prior = Prior + |Prior| × min(max_step, |error|²) × error/|error|
Specific update rules differ per variable type (bounded vs unbounded).
📊
Base vs Incremental Sales
Decomposing total sales into structural and promotional
▶
After modelling, total predicted sales are decomposed into two components:

Base Sales = what would have been sold without any promotional activity (TPR, Lucky Draws, GWP, PWP events).
Incremental Sales = the additional volume driven by promotions = Predicted Sales − Base Sales

Base Sales = exp(α + β_price·Price + β_dist·WTD + β_season·SI + β_trend·TT + ...)
Incremental Sales = Predicted Sales − Base Sales
This split is then applied to actual observed sales (via a % ratio) to give real-world Base_Actual and Incremental_Actual.
🔄
Volume Transferability
Where does volume go when a competitor changes price?
▶
Volume Transferability answers: "If competitor X raises its price by 10%, what fraction of the volume they lose flows to each Carlsberg PPG?"

Algorithm:
  1. For each competitor PPG, find all Carlsberg PPGs that have a cross-price elasticity with that competitor
  2. Compute Volume Gain % = 0.10 × Cross Price Elasticity (simulating a 10% price rise)
  3. Compute absolute Volume Gain = Vol Gain % × actual volume of Carlsberg PPG
  4. Vol Transferability = each PPG's gain / total gain (proportion)
Results are aggregated to Brand-Brand and Internal (Carlsberg vs Carlsberg) vs External (Competitor vs Carlsberg) levels.

🔑 Key Design Decisions

Why two output files?

The "with Promo" file is needed for Feature Engineering to correctly identify non-promo weeks for Base Price calculation. The "without Promo" file is cleaner for everything else.

Why keep Model Flag == 0?

Removing non-modelled PPGs would understate channel-level volume totals. Keeping them as "Others" preserves the volume universe for accurate share calculations.

Feature 1 — Base Price Calculation

Non-Promo Weeks Only 8-Week Rolling Max 3-Pass Smoothing
1

Filter to non-promo weeks

Use the "with Promo" dataset. Filter Promo Flag == 0 (keeping only weeks when the product was NOT on promotion). This ensures the base price isn't contaminated by promotional prices.

If a PPG is always marked as promoted (no non-promo weeks), it's flagged and excluded from base price derivation.
2

8-Week Rolling Maximum (forward + backward)

Two rolling windows are computed and the minimum is taken at each point — this produces a robust "ceiling" price that handles both gradual price increases and price reductions.

# Forward-fill 8-week rolling max (backward-filled for start of series)
bp1 = df.groupby(['Channel','PPG'])['Avg Price'].apply(
    lambda x: x.rolling(8).max()
)
# Backward-looking shifted window (forward-filled for end of series)
bp2 = df.groupby(['Channel','PPG'])['Avg Price'].apply(
    lambda x: x.rolling(8).max().shift(-7)
)
# Take minimum of both → Base_Units_Price_8WR
df['Base_Price_8WR'] = min(bp1, bp2)
3

Smoothing Pass 1 — Cap sudden upward jumps

If base price at week t is higher than both the previous base price AND the current average price, cap it to max(previous base price, current average price). Prevents artificial price spikes.

4

Smoothing Pass 2 — Remove isolated blips

If a point is higher than both its neighbours (isolated spike) OR lower than both its neighbours (isolated dip), replace it with the previous value. Then apply 1% threshold — changes smaller than 1% are flattened.

5

Final correction

Ensure Base Price ≥ Average Price at all times (by definition, base price can never be below average price).

Feature 2 — WTD Smoothing

Same methodology as Base Price

The same 8-week rolling max + 2-pass smoothing pipeline is applied to WTD (Weighted Trading Distribution). This removes artificial distribution spikes during promotional periods and produces a stable underlying distribution trend for modelling.

Feature 3 — TPR (Temporary Price Reduction)

Promotional Depth Variable 
# TPR = discount depth from Base Price
TPR = 1 − (Average Price / Base Price)

# Apply 5% threshold — tiny discounts treated as no discount
TPR = 0 if TPR < 0.05

# Also zero-out TPR if volume < 8-week mean (low-volume weeks)
TPR = 0 if Total_Units < 8_week_rolling_mean_of_units

# Final model variable
LN_TPR = log(1 + TPR)

Feature 4 — Seasonality Index

Week-of-year pattern Savitzky-Golay smoothed

Captures predictable seasonal patterns in beer consumption (e.g., summer peaks, holiday periods):

  1. Sum total volume by Channel × Week-of-year across all PPGs
  2. Compute average weekly volume across the whole dataset
  3. SI = (week's avg volume) / (overall avg volume) — values above 1.0 = above-average weeks
  4. Apply Savitzky-Golay filter (window=15, polynomial order=3) to smooth the curve
Savitzky-Golay is a local polynomial smoothing filter that preserves peaks and troughs better than a simple moving average.

Feature 5 — Stockpiling

Post-promo demand dip

For each discount week, volume in subsequent 1–3 weeks decays by a factor. Multiple decay rates (5%, 10%, 15%, 20%) are computed. The decay rate with the most negative correlation with actual sales is selected per PPG (because stockpiling should depress future sales).

# For decay rate j, for each week i after a promo:
StockPiling_j(t+i) = discount_flag(t) × (1 − j)^(i+1)

# Take max across the 1-3 lag weeks → summarizes the ongoing effect
StockPiling_j = max(StockPiling_j_lag1, lag2, lag3)

# Select the decay rate with min correlation to actual units per PPG
final_stockpiling = argmin(correlation(StockPiling_j, Total Units))

Feature 7 — OOS (Out of Stock)

Distribution-based

Three steps to compute OOS Ratio and OOS Recovery:

  1. Transformed WTD: On promo weeks, WTD is inflated. Transform it by filling forward the non-promo WTD baseline.
  2. ACV Threshold: 30th percentile of Transformed WTD during stable distribution periods (when the ACV Reach sign is 0, i.e., no significant change).
  3. OOS = max(0, ACV_Threshold − Transformed_WTD) — if current distribution is below threshold, the product is OOS.
  4. OOS_Recovery = negative of week-on-week change in OOS_Ratio when OOS is falling (stock coming back).

Other Features

⏳ Time Trend

Assigns a sequential period number to each unique week. Time_Trend = 1 + (Period_Number / 100). Captures long-run secular trends in category consumption.

📦 Downsizing

Detects if a PPG's effective pack size has changed over time (e.g., 330ml → 320ml same price). Computed as (Total Volume × 1000) / Total Units rounded to nearest 2% of nominal pack size.

# This captures the "recent ceiling" price looking backward

# Formula: 1 − (Current Avg Price / Base Price)
# Example: Base Price = 10, Avg Price = 8.5  →  TPR = 0.15  (15% discount)
df_bp['TPR'] = df_bp.apply(
    lambda x: 0 if x['Base Price'] == 0
              else (1 - (x['Average Price (Unit)'] / x['Base Price'])), axis=1
)

# Apply 5% threshold — discounts below 5% are noise, not real promotions
discount_threshold = 0.05
df_bp['TPR'] = df_bp['TPR'].apply(lambda x: 0 if x < discount_threshold else x)

# Also zero out TPR if volume in that week is below the 8-week rolling mean
# Rationale: a "discount" week with below-average volume is likely not a real promo
# (could be a stocking issue or data artifact)
df_bp['8 weeks rolling mean'] = df_bp['Total Units'].rolling(window=8).mean().fillna(0)
df_bp['TPR'] = df_bp.apply(
    lambda x: 1 if x['Total Units'] < x['8 weeks rolling mean'] else x['TPR'], axis=1

    temp['Pack Size'],    # within 2% tolerance → use nominal pack size (no change)
    temp['Downsizing']    # outside tolerance → keep measured value (downsizing detected)
)

Section 9 — Stockpiling (Post-Promo Demand Dip)

# Stockpiling: consumers buy extra during a promo and buy less in the
# 1-3 weeks after. This depresses future sales after a promo ends.

tpr_threshold     = 0.95      # TPR ratio ≥ 0.95 means no meaningful discount — StockUp = 0
stockpiling_weeks = 3         # effect lasts up to 3 weeks after promo

# Step 1: Mark the WEEK AFTER a promo week as StockUp = 1
df['StockUp'] = 0.0
df.loc[df['TPR'] >= tpr_threshold, 'StockUp'] = 1.0
# Shift StockUp by 1 week — the dip happens the week AFTER the discount
StockUp = df.groupby('key').StockUp.apply(lambda x: x.rolling(1).mean().shift(1))
df['StockUp'] = StockUp
# Clear StockUp on weeks that are themselves also promo weeks
df.loc[(df['StockUp'] > 0) & (df['TPR'] >= tpr_threshold), 'StockUp'] = 0.0

# Step 2: Try 4 different decay rates: 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%
# For each decay rate j, compute how much demand was "stolen" k weeks ago:
# StockPiling_j at (t+k) = StockUp(t) × (1 - j)^(k+1)
# Higher decay = demand recovers faster after the promo
for j in [0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20]:
    for i in range(0, stockpiling_weeks):               # i = 0,1,2 → 1,2,3 weeks after promo
        decay = (1 - j) ** (i + 1)                      # exponential decay: e.g. 0.95^1, 0.95^2, 0.95^3
        df['StockUp_' + str(i)] = df['StockUp'] * decay
        # Shift this lag i+1 weeks into the future
        df['StockUp_' + str(i)] = df.groupby('key')['StockUp_' + str(i)].apply(
            lambda x: x.rolling(1).mean().shift(i + 1)
        )
    # Take the max across the 3 lag periods → single stockpiling effect per week
    df['StockPiling' + str(round(j,2))] = df[['StockUp','StockUp_0','StockUp_1','StockUp_2']].max(axis=1)

# Step 3: Auto-select the decay rate with the MOST NEGATIVE correlation with actual sales
# Stockpiling should suppress sales → we want the version most negatively correlated
stockpiling_corr = temp.groupby('key').corr()[['Total Units']]
stockpiling_corr['final'] = stockpiling_corr[stockpiling_columns].idxmin(axis=1)  # most negative

# Assign the best decay rate's stockpiling values as the final StockPiling feature
df['StockPiling'] = 0.0
for decay_col in df['final'].unique():
    df.loc[df['final'] == decay_col, 'StockPiling'] = df[decay_col]

Section 10 — OOS (Out of Stock)

# We want the "true" underlying distribution — not the promo-inflated one.

# treat those weeks as "non-promo" for distribution baseline (avoid data gaps)

    temp_df.groupby(temp_df['week_type'].diff().ne(0).cumsum())['week_type']
    .transform('size').lt(12)
)

# Fill in non-promo WTD for all weeks → forward/backward fill to cover promo weeks
temp_df.loc[temp_df['after_12wk_week_type'] == 0.0, 'wtd_nopromo'] = temp_df['WTD']

df_oos['TransformedWTD'] = df_oos['WTD']   # start with actual WTD, then override

# ── Step 2: ACV Threshold = 30th percentile of TransformedWTD ───────────────
# During stable distribution periods (ACV Reach sign = 1),
# compute the 30th percentile WTD. This is the "normal minimum distribution".
# If current WTD drops below this → product is Out of Stock.
df['Change in ACV Reach'] = df.groupby(['Channel','PPG'])['TransformedWTD'].pct_change().fillna(0)
# ACV Reach sign = 1 means stable (change < 3%), = 0 means significant change
df['ACV Reach sign'] = np.where(df['Change in ACV Reach'].abs() >= 0.03, 0, 1)
df['ACV_Threshold'] = df.groupby(
    ['Channel','PPG', df['ACV Reach sign'].diff().ne(0).cumsum()]
)['TransformedWTD'].transform(lambda x: x.quantile(0.3))   # 30th percentile per stable segment

# ── Step 3: Compute OOS_Ratio and OOS_Recovery ──────────────────────────────
# OOS = how much distribution is MISSING below the normal baseline
# = max(0, ACV_Threshold − TransformedWTD)  →  capped at 0 if no stock-out
df['OOS'] = np.maximum(0, df['ACV_Threshold'] - df['TransformedWTD'])
df['OOS_Ratio'] = df['OOS'] / 100   # scale to a 0–1 range

# OOS_Recovery = week-on-week improvement in OOS (OOS ratio going down = stock recovering)
# Use the negative of the change → positive value means stock is coming back
df['OOS_Recovery'] = df.groupby(['Channel','PPG'])['OOS_Ratio'].diff()
df.loc[df['OOS_Recovery'] >= 0, 'OOS_Recovery'] = 0.0   # only keep negative changes (recovery)
df['OOS_Recovery'] = df['OOS_Recovery'] * (-1)            # flip sign → positive = recovering

3. Competition.py

Builds a binary competitor mapping matrix — for each Carlsberg PPG, which other PPGs are considered its competitors?

Competition.py

📥 Input: Master Data (without Promo) + Competition Rules Excel
📤 Output: Comp_Matrix (CVS & SMKT separate + merged file)
📥 Inputs

Master Data (no Promo) · Competition Rules Excel (3 sheets: Segment interaction, Pack Size×Count, ABV Classification)

📤 Output

Competition Rules Logic

Two PPGs are considered competitors if and only if all three rules are satisfied simultaneously:

Rule 1: Beer Segment

Segments that are "close enough" to compete — e.g., Premium vs Super-Premium may compete, but Economy and Premium may not. Defined in a cross-segment interaction matrix.

Rule 2: ABV Classification

Alcohol content bands. Regular, Mid-Strength, and Strong beers may or may not compete with each other depending on the interaction matrix.

Rule 3: Pack Size × Pack Count

Pack format (Single vs Multipack, Regular/King/Super-King sizes). CVS uses Single vs Multipack; SMKT uses 1/4/6/12 pack categories with more granular rules.

Competition flag = 1 only when Flag1=1 AND Flag2=1 AND Flag3=1. This AND-logic ensures only truly substitutable products are modelled as competitors.

Step-by-Step Process

1

Prepare PPG attributes

Classify each PPG into Pack Size Range (Regular <440ml / King 440–500ml / Super King >500ml) and Pack Count Range. Derive BEER_SEGMENT from Segment column and ABV Class from ABV Classification.

2

Convert interaction matrices to long format

The Excel input files have interaction matrices in wide format (rows = target type, columns = competitor type, values = 0/1 flag). These are melted to long format for easier joining.

3

Create full PPG pair list

All PPG pairs within a channel are generated. Then the pair list is made symmetric — if A competes with B, then B also competes with A.

4

Merge and apply all three rules

The three interaction flags are joined to the PPG pair list. Final Competition Inclusion Flag = 1 only when all three intermediate flags = 1.

5

Pivot to matrix format

Convert from long format (Target, Competitor, Flag) to wide matrix format (rows = Target PPG, columns = Competitor PPGs, values = 0/1). This is the format consumed by the Modelling script.

📌 Important Notes

  • The script runs separately for CVS and SMKT — CVS uses "Single/Multipack" pack count rules while SMKT uses granular 1/4/6/12 count buckets.
  • A merged matrix (Comp_matrix_HK.xlsx) combines both channels into one file.
  • PPGs not mapped to any competitor (due to uniqueness in category) are flagged in a missing_comp dataframe — these receive zero cross-price elasticity in modelling.
  • Only the last 1 year (52 weeks) of data is used for computing competition mapping (recent market structure).

Step 1 of 2: OLS — Variable Selection & Generating Initial Priors

First half of Modeling.py — Sections 1–10

The first half of Modeling.py is entirely the OLS section. It reads two inputs — the Feature Engineering output and the Competition Matrix — and builds a clean variable list per PPG, runs OLS regression with a 4-step variable selection process, clips the resulting coefficients to economic bounds, and saves the output to ModelEQ.xlsx (the starting point for GDA).

Part A — Building the ModelEQ (variable list per PPG)

1

All price, distribution, and TPR variables are log-transformed. The dataframe is melted from wide (one column per variable) to long (one row per PPG × Variable × Date). 2

Build ModelEQ — merge Competition Matrix with FE variables

The Competition Matrix (which PPG competes with which) is merged with the FE variable list to create one row per PPG × variable. Each row is tagged Own or Competitor based on whether the PPG name appears in the variable name. Then inappropriate variables are dropped:

  • Own variables removed: LN_Average_Units_Price, LN_Comp_WTD (own PPG doesn't compete with itself)
  • Competitor variables removed: LN_WTD, LN_Base_Units_Price, LN_TPR, OOS, StockPiling, SeasonalityIndex, event flags (competitors only contribute price and distribution)

The SD column is set to a fixed constant of 0.01 for all rows — it is not derived from OLS.

Part B — 4-Step Variable Selection before running OLS

For each PPG, variables go through four sequential filters before OLS is fit. Variables are dropped at each step if they fail the check:

①

Covariance Check

Drop any variable where the absolute covariance with log(sales) is < 0.001. This removes variables that have essentially zero linear relationship with the dependent variable. Exception: LN_Base_Units_Price is always kept even if it fails this check.

②

Correlation with Base Price (threshold = 0.6)

For competitor variables only (LN_Average_Units_Price, LN_Comp_WTD): drop if |Pearson correlation with own Base Price| > 0.6. This prevents multicollinearity — a competitor's price that moves in lockstep with own base price cannot be identified separately.

③

Correlation with Sales (threshold = 0.6)

For competitor variables only: drop if |Pearson correlation with log(sales)| > 0.6. If a competitor variable is already strongly correlated with sales, including it alongside own variables causes instability.

④

Stepwise Backward Elimination (p-value threshold = 0.2)

Run OLS with all remaining variables. Iteratively drop the variable with the highest p-value if it exceeds 0.2. Repeat until all remaining variables have p-value ≤ 0.2. This is stepwise_selection() using statsmodels.OLS.

worst_pval = pvalues.max()
if worst_pval > 0.2:
    X.drop(pvalues.idxmax(), axis=1)  # remove worst variable
# repeat until all p-values ≤ 0.2

Part C — OLS fit & prior clipping

After variable selection, sm.OLS(Y, X).fit() is run. The resulting coefficients become the Prior column. These are then clipped to economic bounds before saving:

  • StockPiling > 0 → set to 0 (must be negative; post-promo dip)
  • SeasonalityIndex ≤ 0 → set to 0 (must be positive or zero)
  • Promo event variables ≤ 0 → set to 0 (Lucky Draw, GWP, PWP must be positive)
  • Own price, WTD, TPR, OOS, Competitor price/distribution → all clipped to their defined bounds

Step 2 of 2: GDA — Constrained Refinement

100 iterations per PPG

The GDA takes the OLS priors from ModelEQ.xlsx and refines them over 100 iterations, enforcing all economic constraints. Each iteration computes predicted sales, measures error, correlates error with each variable, and nudges the coefficients in the right direction — all within the allowed bounds.

Economic Constraints / Bounds

All coefficients are constrained to economically meaningful ranges. The GDA never allows a coefficient to violate these bounds:

VariableLower BoundUpper BoundRationale
Own Price (LN_Base_Units_Price)−4.0−0.01Must be negative (demand law). Max of −4 is very elastic.
Distribution (LN_WTD)+0.01+4.0More distribution = more sales (positive).
TPR (LN_TPR)−15.0−0.01Promotion drives volume (negative coeff = more discount → more sales).
Competitor Price (LN_Avg_Price)+0.001+0.75 × CCFCompetitor price rise = volume gain (positive). Capped by Competition Coeff Factor.
Competitor Distribution (LN_Comp_WTD)−0.5 × CCF−0.001Competitor gaining distribution = our volume loss (negative).
OOS Ratio−4.0−0.01OOS depresses sales (negative).
OOS Recovery+0.01+2.0OOS recovering = sales bouncing back (positive).
Downsizing+0.01+2.0Pack size reduction → apparent price cut → volume gain.

Competition Coefficient Factor (CCF)

The upper bound for cross-price elasticities is not fixed — it scales with the relative volume of the competitor PPG vs the target PPG:

# CCF ensures a competitor's impact cannot exceed its relative market size
CCF = competitor_PPG_volume / (competitor_PPG_volume + target_PPG_volume)

A large competitor (high CCF) can have a bigger impact on your sales than a tiny one. This prevents economically impossible large cross-price elasticities.

GDA Core Algorithm — 7 Functions

1

createdata() — Build X and Y matrices

For a given PPG, pivots the RawData long-format dataframe into a wide matrix: columns = variables, rows = weeks. Y = log(actual sales). X = log-transformed feature values. Missing X values are filled with 0.

2

setting_priors() — Initialize/validate priors

Converts the prior dictionary. Applies a special rule: OOS Recovery prior cannot exceed OOS Ratio prior in absolute magnitude (recovery ≤ original impact).

3

calculating_alpha() — Compute intercept

The intercept α = mean(Y) − mean(Ŷ). This ensures the model's predicted mean equals the actual mean — a fundamental requirement for an unbiased model.

4

ComputePredictedSales() — Forward pass

Computes Ŷ = α + Σ(coefficient_i × X_i) for all weeks. Stores results per iteration.

5

ComputeErrorandCorrelation() — Backward pass

error = exp(Y_actual) − exp(Ŷ). Then computes Pearson correlation of each variable with this error. Variables positively correlated with error should have their coefficient increased; negatively correlated should be decreased.

6

AdjustandUpdatePriors() — Constrained update step

Updates each coefficient within its bounds:

# General update rule (for unbounded variables):
New_Prior = Prior + |Prior| × min(max_step, error²) × error/|error|

# For bounded variables (e.g., own price, negative bound):
# If error < 0: push toward lower bound
New_Prior = Prior + max((LL − Prior)/2, |Prior| × step × error/|error|)
# If error > 0: push toward upper bound
New_Prior = min(UL − (UL − Prior)/2, Prior + |Prior| × step × error/|error|)

The Slowdown Factor is initialized at 2.0 and increases by 1.2× each time a variable's correlation sign oscillates between consecutive iterations — preventing it from "bouncing" and not converging.

7

calculateBaseline() — Base vs Incremental decomposition

Reconstructs predicted sales using only "base" variables (excluding TPR and all event flags). The remaining = Incremental Sales. Both are then applied to actual observed sales using a proportional split.

Model Iteration Pruning

At iterations 15, 45, and 70, weak variables are pruned (set to 0.0):

  • Competitor price variables with coefficients <0.005 → dropped
  • Competitor distribution variables with |coefficient| <0.005 → dropped
  • Seasonality and Stockpiling with |coefficient| <0.005 → dropped
This pruning prevents over-fitting and ensures only variables with meaningful impact are retained in the final model.

Model Quality Metrics

R² (R-Squared)

Proportion of variance in actual sales explained by the model. Target: R² ≥ 0.70. Computed from linear regression of predicted vs actual on the final (100th) iteration.

MAPE (Mean Absolute % Error)

Average absolute percentage error of predictions. Target: MAPE ≤ 25–30%. Lower is better.

        # ── Filter 3: Competitor correlation with Sales (threshold = 0.6) ───────
        # A competitor variable already highly correlated with sales would
        # "steal" explanatory power from own variables and destabilize the model
        for comp_var in comp_cols:
            corr = abs(Y.corr(X[comp_var]))
            if corr <= 0.6:
                keep.append(comp_var)

        # ── Filter 4: Stepwise Backward Elimination (p-value threshold = 0.2) ───
        # Run OLS iteratively. Each round: check all p-values.
        # If the worst p-value exceeds 0.2 → that variable is statistically insignificant → drop it
        # Repeat until ALL remaining variables have p-value ≤ 0.2
        model = sm.OLS(y, sm.add_constant(X)).fit()
        pvalues = model.pvalues.iloc[1:]               # exclude intercept
        while pvalues.max() > 0.2:
            worst_feature = pvalues.idxmax()
            X.drop(columns=[worst_feature], inplace=True)   # drop the weakest variable
            model = sm.OLS(y, sm.add_constant(X)).fit()     # refit without it
            pvalues = model.pvalues.iloc[1:]

        # ── Final OLS fit ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
        X_final = sm.add_constant(X)
        ols = sm.OLS(Y, X_final).fit()
        Comp_Matrix = ols.params             # regression coefficients (one per variable)
        dict = Comp_Matrix.to_dict()
        del dict['const']                    # drop the intercept — GDA handles it separately
        # Each PPG's coefficients → added to ModelEQ as 'Prior' column
        coefficient = pd.DataFrame(list(dict.items()), columns=['Variable','Prior'])

calculating_alpha() — Compute intercept each iteration

def calculating_alpha(X, Y, priors_dict):
    # Compute predicted log-sales using current coefficient values
    X['Predicted'] = 0
    for column in X.columns[:-1]:           # exclude 'Predicted' column itself
        X['Predicted'] += X[column] * priors_dict[column]

    # Intercept α = mean(actual) − mean(predicted)
    # This ensures the model is "centered" — predicted mean = actual mean
    # Without this, the model could be systematically above or below actual sales
    mu_intercept = np.nanmean(Y) - np.nanmean(X['Predicted'])
    return mu_intercept, X

def AdjustandUpdatePriors(temp, priorsdata, newlist, itr, ...):
    max_step = 0.15   # maximum fraction to move the coefficient in one iteration

    # ── Slowdown factor: prevents oscillation ─────────────────────────────────
    # Start at 2.0 for first 4 iterations (conservative step size)
    # If a variable's correlation sign flips between 3 consecutive iterations
    # (oscillating: +/-/+) → multiply slowdown by 1.2 to dampen the movement
    if itr < 4:
        newlist['Slow_down_factor'] = 2.0
    if itr >= 4:
        # detect sign oscillation in correlation across last 3 iterations
        newlist.loc[
            (newlist['Correlation'+str(itr-1)] > 0) &
            (newlist['Correlation'+str(itr-2)] < 0) &
            (newlist['Correlation'+str(itr-3)] > 0),
            'Slow_down_factor'
        ] *= 1.2

    # ── Step size formula ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    # step = min(max_step, |error| / Slow_down_factor)
    # Limits how far we move the coefficient — large errors move it more
    step = np.minimum(max_step, abs(temp['error']) / temp['Slow_down_factor'])

    # ── Update rule for BOUNDED variables (e.g. own price, bounded to [−4.0, −0.01]) ──
    # If error < 0 (predicted too high): push coefficient toward lower bound
    #   New = Prior + max((LL − Prior)/2, |Prior| × step × direction)
    #   The (LL - Prior)/2 term ensures we never overshoot the lower bound
    temp.loc[(temp['Prior'] < 0) & (temp['error'] < 0) &
             (temp['Variable'].str.contains('LN_Base_Units_Price')), 'New Prior'] = (
        temp['Prior'] + np.maximum(
            (base_price[0] - temp['Prior']) / 2,     # half-step toward lower bound
            abs(temp['Prior']) * step * temp['error'] / abs(temp['error'])
        )
    )
    # If error > 0 (predicted too low): push coefficient toward upper bound
    #   New = min(UL − (UL − Prior)/2, Prior + |Prior| × step × direction)
    temp.loc[(temp['Prior'] < 0) & (temp['error'] > 0) &
             (temp['Variable'].str.contains('LN_Base_Units_Price')), 'New Prior'] = np.minimum(
        base_price[1] - (base_price[1] - temp['Prior']) / 2,   # half-step toward upper bound
        temp['Prior'] + abs(temp['Prior']) * step * temp['error'] / abs(temp['error'])
    )

    # ── Update rule for UNBOUNDED variables (Seasonality, Stockpiling) ────────
    # New = Prior + |Prior| × min(max_step, error²) × error/|error|
    # The error² term makes updates larger when errors are large and smaller when errors are small
    temp.loc[~(is_bounded_variable), 'New Prior'] = (
        temp['Prior'] + abs(temp['Prior']) *
        np.minimum(max_step, temp['error']**2) *
        temp['error'] / abs(temp['error'])
    )

    # ── Pruning at iterations 15, 45, 70 ──────────────────────────────────────
    # Variables with near-zero coefficients at these checkpoints are dropped (set to 0)
    # Prevents overfitting — weak variables should not be kept just because they started non-zero
    if itr in [15, 45, 70]:
        temp.loc[(temp['Variable'].str.contains('LN_Average_Units_Price')) &
                 (temp['Prior'] < 0.005), 'Prior'] = 0.0
        temp.loc[(temp['Variable'].str.contains('SeasonalityIndex')) &
                 (temp['Prior'] < 0.005), 'Prior'] = 0.0
